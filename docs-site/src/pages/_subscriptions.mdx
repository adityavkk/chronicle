<Tldr>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>A subscription is a durable cursor per stream.</strong> When a linked stream's tail moves past its <code>acked_offset</code>, the subscription has pending work and the server wakes a worker.</li>
    <li><strong>Two delivery modes share one model.</strong> <code>webhook</code> signs and POSTs a notification to your URL. <code>pull-wake</code> writes a wake event to a stream your workers poll and claim. Both use the same cursor, generation, lease, and stream membership.</li>
    <li><strong>The generation fence keeps duplicates safe.</strong> Every wake carries a monotonic <code>generation</code> and a single-use <code>wake_id</code>. A stale callback gets <code>409 FENCED</code>. Delivery is at-least-once, and the fence makes a duplicate harmless.</li>
    <li><strong>All state is in Redis, under one hash tag.</strong> The reference engines keep cursors, leases, and retry schedules in an in-memory map that a restart loses. Chronicle persists them under <code>{"{__ds}"}</code> so they survive a restart. It also runs a recovery sweep, because after a crash a durable cursor for an idle subscription is never re-checked on its own.</li>
    <li><strong>Checked under fault injection.</strong> A Jepsen-style suite kills origins and Redis mid-flight on Kubernetes. Every durably appended message still reaches its cursor.</li>
  </ul>
</Tldr>

<section class="block" id="model">
  <SectionHead num="01" label="Model" title="Durable cursors that <em>wake workers</em>" />

  A subscription links one or more streams and tracks an `acked_offset` for each.
  That offset is the last offset a worker has processed, inclusive.[^protocol]
  When an append pushes a linked stream's tail past its cursor, the subscription
  has *pending work*. The server then issues a **wake**, a unit of delivery that
  carries a snapshot of which streams are behind and by how much. A worker
  processes the events and acks the new offsets, the cursor advances, and the
  subscription goes idle until the next append. Nothing replays history by
  default. A new link starts at the stream's current tail.

  A subscription delivers wakes one of two ways, chosen at creation by `type`.

  <Table>
    | | `webhook` | `pull-wake` |
    | --- | --- | --- |
    | Who initiates delivery | the **server** POSTs to your `webhook.url` | your **workers** poll a `wake_stream` and claim |
    | Wake carrier | a signed HTTP notification | a `{type:"wake"}` event appended to a durable stream |
    | When the lease arms | at wake issue | at claim |
    | Auth on the work | the server signs (Ed25519), and you verify | a service JWT to claim, then a claim token to ack |
    | Best for | push to a known endpoint behind TLS | a worker pool draining at its own pace |
  </Table>

  Both modes share one cursor, generation, lease, and membership model. Only the
  carrier and when the lease arms differ.

  <Aside label="Durability gap" summary="Why both reference servers lose subscription state on restart">
    The protocol calls subscriptions <em>durable cursors</em> and requires their
    state to survive a server restart, meaning the cursor, the retry schedule, and
    the lease.[^research] Both reference implementations, the TypeScript dev server
    and the Go Caddy plugin, keep all of it in an in-memory map and lose it on
    restart. The log already persists to Redis. Chronicle persists the
    subscription layer there too, and a sweep re-evaluates what is owed.
  </Aside>
</section>

<section class="block" id="webhook">
  <SectionHead num="02" label="Webhook" title="Sign, POST, <em>auto-ack</em>" />

  Create a webhook subscription with a `PUT`. The `pattern` is a glob over paths
  relative to the stream root, where `*` matches one segment and `**` matches
  zero or more. Matching streams are linked at their current tail. The response
  echoes the signing metadata but never a shared secret, because deliveries are
  signed with an asymmetric key.

  ```http title="Register a webhook subscription"
  PUT /v1/stream/__ds/subscriptions/sub-1 HTTP/1.1
  Content-Type: application/json

  {"type":"webhook","pattern":"events/*",
   "webhook":{"url":"https://worker.example/hooks"},
   "lease_ttl_ms":30000}

  HTTP/1.1 201 Created
  Content-Type: application/json

  {"id":"sub-1","type":"webhook","status":"active",
   "streams":[],
   "webhook":{"url":"https://worker.example/hooks",
     "signing":{"alg":"ed25519","kid":"ds_abc123",
       "jwks_url":"https://server.example/v1/stream/__ds/jwks.json"}}}
  ```

  <Aside label="Edge case" summary="Which webhook URLs the server rejects (SSRF guard)">
    Webhook URLs are checked to prevent server-side request forgery. Production
    targets must be `https`. The server rejects RFC1918, link-local, loopback by
    DNS, and cloud-metadata addresses with `400 WEBHOOK_URL_REJECTED`.[^ssrf] A
    literal loopback host is allowed in development. A trusted-network flag
    accepts private targets for receivers inside the cluster.
  </Aside>

  When a matching append lands and the subscription is idle, the server bumps the
  `generation`, mints a `wake_id`, arms the lease, and POSTs a signed
  notification. The `Webhook-Signature` is an Ed25519 signature over
  `<timestamp>.<raw_body>`, and the wake body's `kid` selects the verifying key
  from the JWKS.

  ```http title="The wake (server → your receiver)"
  POST https://worker.example/hooks HTTP/1.1
  Content-Type: application/json
  Webhook-Signature: t=1778324210,kid=ds_abc123,ed25519=<base64url>

  {"subscription_id":"sub-1","wake_id":"w_abc123","generation":7,
   "streams":[{"path":"events/abc","link_type":"glob",
     "acked_offset":"...0042","tail_offset":"...0084","has_pending":true}],
   "callback_url":"https://server.example/v1/stream/__ds/subscriptions/sub-1/callback",
   "callback_token":"eyJ..."}
  ```

  Your receiver can finish two ways. Return `{done:true}` and the server
  **auto-acks** the tails in that snapshot and releases the lease, so the whole
  round trip is one POST. Or return `200`, process the work later, then call back
  to `callback_url` with the offsets you reached. A non-2xx response or a
  transport error schedules a retry with exponential backoff from 1 s to 60 s and
  20% jitter. The retry schedule is persisted, so a restart honors it.

<section class="block" id="fence">
  <SectionHead num="03" label="Fence" title="Generation is the <em>fence</em>, not the lease" />

  A subscription moves through three phases. **Idle** means no wake is in flight
  and no lease is held. **Waking** means a wake was issued but not yet claimed or
  completed. **Live** means a worker holds the lease and extends it by heartbeats.
  The lease bounds how long a worker may hold a wake. When it expires the server
  clears the holder, and if work remains it issues a fresh wake.

  Correctness does not rest on the lease. A time-based lease cannot fence a worker
  that has only stalled, so the server treats delivery as **at-least-once** and
  uses a monotonic token instead.[^fence] Every callback, ack, and release is
  rejected `409 FENCED` unless all of these match the current state.

  <Table>
    | Check | Must equal |
    | --- | --- |
    | Bearer token | a valid token for this subscription |
    | token `generation` | the current generation |
    | request `generation` | the current generation |
    | request `wake_id` | the current `wake_id` (non-empty) |
  </Table>

  A duplicate or late callback loses the comparison and is rejected, which is what
  makes at-least-once safe. A wake may be delivered more than once, by a lease
  reclaim, by a retry, or by a sweep, and the second delivery is harmless.

  ```http title="Ack a wake, then watch a replay get fenced"
  POST /v1/stream/__ds/subscriptions/sub-1/callback HTTP/1.1
  Authorization: Bearer eyJ...
  Content-Type: application/json

  {"wake_id":"w_abc123","generation":7,
   "acks":[{"stream":"events/abc","offset":"...0084"}],"done":true}

  HTTP/1.1 200 OK
  {"ok":true,"next_wake":false}

  ← a replay of the same body, after the wake was cleared:
  HTTP/1.1 409 Conflict
  {"error":{"code":"FENCED"}}
  ```

  Not every rejection is a fence. A callback, ack, or release can also fail these
  ways, each with a distinct code so a client can tell a stale replay from a bad
  request, an aged-out token, or a subscription that no longer exists:

  <Table>
    | Status | Code | When |
    | --- | --- | --- |
    | `400` | `INVALID_REQUEST` | the body is missing `generation` or `wake_id` (an explicit `0`/`""` is not missing — it reaches the fence and loses as `409`) |
    | `401` | `TOKEN_EXPIRED` | the Bearer token has expired — the response body carries a **fresh** token; retry with it |
    | `409` | `FENCED` | the token/request `generation` or `wake_id` no longer matches (the fence above) |
    | `409` | `ALREADY_CLAIMED` | a pull-wake `claim` while the lease is already held |
    | `410` | `SUBSCRIPTION_GONE` | the subscription was deleted |
  </Table>

  A long-lived worker never has to re-claim just because its token aged out. On a
  successful ack whose token is within the refresh window the server **rolls the
  token forward in-band**, returning it as an optional `token` on the `{"ok":…}`
  body — adopt it for the next call. If a token does expire first, the
  `401 TOKEN_EXPIRED` body carries a fresh one, so the worker recovers on the spot
  instead of stalling until its lease lapses.

  ```http title="A refreshing ack returns the next token; an expired one hands back a fresh token"
  HTTP/1.1 200 OK
  {"ok":true,"next_wake":false,"token":"eyJ...new"}

  ← when the presented token had already expired:
  HTTP/1.1 401 Unauthorized
  {"error":{"code":"TOKEN_EXPIRED"},"token":"eyJ...fresh"}
  ```

<section class="block" id="pull-wake">
  <SectionHead num="04" label="Pull-wake" title="Claim a <em>subscription-scoped lease</em>" />

  A pull-wake subscription names a `wake_stream`, an ordinary durable stream the
  application creates. Instead of POSTing, the server appends a small wake event
  to that stream. A pool of workers tails it and races to **claim** the
  subscription. The claim is a compare-and-set on the lease. The first worker wins
  and gets a snapshot plus a claim token. A second worker that tries while the
  lease is held gets `409 ALREADY_CLAIMED` naming the current holder.

  ```http title="Claim, then ack"
  POST /v1/stream/__ds/subscriptions/sub-1/claim HTTP/1.1
  Content-Type: application/json

  {"worker":"worker-1"}

  HTTP/1.1 200 OK
  {"wake_id":"w_abc123","generation":7,"token":"eyJ...","lease_ttl_ms":30000,
   "streams":[{"path":"events/abc","link_type":"glob",
     "acked_offset":"...0042","tail_offset":"...0084","has_pending":true}]}

  ← a second worker, while the lease is held:
  HTTP/1.1 409 Conflict
  {"error":{"code":"ALREADY_CLAIMED","current_holder":"worker-1","generation":7}}
  ```

  The worker acks through the same fenced endpoint as a webhook callback. It sends
  `done:true` to finish, or omits it to heartbeat and keep the lease. A worker can
  also `release` without acking. If work remains, the server writes another wake
  event for the next claim.

<section class="block" id="durability">
  <SectionHead num="05" label="Durability" title="Why a cursor <em>isn't enough</em>" />

  The whole control plane lives in Redis under one fixed hash tag, `{__ds}`, so
  every correctness-critical script touches one slot and is safe to run on a
  cluster. The per-stream log has high cardinality and stays sharded under each
  stream's own tag. Only the control plane is centralized, and it holds far less
  state, one entry per subscription rather than one per message.

  <Table>
    | Key | Type | Holds |
    | --- | --- | --- |
    | `ds:{__ds}:sub:<id>` | hash | config + runtime: `generation`, `wake_id`, `phase`, `holder`, `lease_until_ns`, retry state |
    | `ds:{__ds}:sub:<id>:links` | hash | the durable cursors, `path → "<link_type>:<acked_offset>"` |
    | `ds:{__ds}:subs` | set | every subscription id, for the sweep |
    | `ds:{__ds}:stream:<path>` | set | fan-out index: which subscriptions a stream wakes |
    | `ds:{__ds}:sched:lease` | sorted set | `sub → lease_expiry_ns`, the liveness lease as a due-scored timer |
    | `ds:{__ds}:sched:retry` | sorted set | `sub → next_attempt_ns`, the webhook backoff schedule |
    | `ds:{__ds}:jwks` | hash | Ed25519 signing keys, so the `kid` is stable across restarts |
  </Table>

  <Aside label="Cost model" summary="The schedule ZSETs, the fan-out set read, and the symbol glossary">

    ```text title="The schedule is a due-scored ZSET that survives a crash"
    ZADD :sched:lease (now + ttl) sub           # arm a lease        O(log L)
    due = ZRANGEBYSCORE :sched:lease -inf now    # what is overdue?   O(log L + D)
    for sub in due:
        ZADD :sched:lease (now + visibility) sub  # re-score forward   O(log L)
    # claim-by-re-score, never ZREM: a worker that dies leaves the item to
    # fall due again. L = scheduled subscriptions, D = due this tick.
    ```

    ```text title="Wake fan-out is a SET read, and the sweep is the one O(K) pass"
    SMEMBERS :stream:<path>             # the F subs this append wakes   O(F)
    for sub in those: arm_wake(sub)     # each O(log L)                  => O(F log L)

    for id in SMEMBERS :subs:           # every subscription, each tick  O(K)
        expire_lease(id); if behind: arm_wake(id)   # each O(log L)
    # => O(K log L) per sweep — linear in subscription count, not log size.
    ```

    Throughout: **K** = subscriptions, **P** = streams linked to one
    subscription, **F** = subscriptions a stream wakes, **L** = entries in a
    schedule ZSET, **D** = entries due in a tick, **A** = streams acked in one
    request.[^cost]
  </Aside>

  Persisting the cursor is necessary but **not sufficient**. A wake is created
  only by a new append or by an in-flight wake ending its lifecycle. After a
  restart, an *idle* subscription whose streams are quiet is never re-evaluated.
  Its durable cursor now makes the answer computable, but nothing asks the
  question.[^research] So Chronicle runs a **recovery sweep** on boot and on the
  sweep interval, a 30 s coarse recovery floor. The append-time hook is the fast
  path. Recovery is event-triggered, so the floor is a backstop, not the primary
  path. The sweep walks every subscription, expires stale leases, and for any idle
  subscription with pending work it re-issues the wake the crash dropped.

  <Complexity
    label="Complexity: the sweep is the one linear pass"
    time="O(K · (P + log L)) per tick"
    space="O(K) ids + O(P) per subscription"
    why="The sweep is the only O(K) operation in the system. It runs <code>SMEMBERS</code> over all K subscriptions and, for each, loads its state and cursors (O(P) for P links), expires a stale lease (O(log L)), compares each cursor against its stream's tail, and re-arms if behind.   The lease and retry schedules are due-scored ZSETs, claimed by a script that
  **re-scores** due members forward rather than removing them. A worker that dies
  after taking a due item leaves it to fall due again and be reclaimed. This is
  at-least-once with no leader election. Single-threaded Redis makes exactly one
  origin's re-score win per tick.

    time="O(log L + D log L)"
    space="O(D)"
    why="The due query is one <code>ZRANGEBYSCORE</code>, O(log L) to locate the boundary and O(D) to collect the D overdue members. Re-scoring each forward is an O(log L) <code>ZADD</code>. Nothing is removed, so a claimer that dies costs only a re-fire on the next tick. D is bounded by a per-tick limit, so one claim stays cheap even when many entries fall due at once."
  />

  <Callout icon="alert" label="Durability honesty">
    The guarantee is at-least-once and fenced, not exactly-once. The loss window
    is set by the fsync policy. <code>appendfsync everysec</code> can lose about 1
    to 2 s of writes on a hard crash, which would show up as a stream stuck below
    its tail. Honoring the protocol's MUSTs means <code>appendfsync always</code>
    and <code>WAITAOF</code> to a replica. Even then a failover can drop the last
    unsynced write. That is why the design relies on the fence rather than on
    never losing a write.
  </Callout>
</section>

<section class="block" id="use-cases">
  <SectionHead num="06" label="End to end" title="Two flows, <em>start to finish</em>" />

  The parts above combine into the two shapes you actually deploy. One is a
  **push** to a service that owns an endpoint. The other is a **pull** by a pool
  that drains at its own pace.

  ### A webhook receipt service <em>(push)</em>

  A notification service has one job, to email a receipt when an order is paid or
  shipped. It registers a single webhook subscription with `pattern: "orders/**"`
  and its receiver URL. Every order stream it will ever care about, including ones
  created later, is matched at its tail. From then on it is passive, and the
  server initiates each delivery.

  When the order service appends `paid` to `orders/A1234`, that append gets its
  `204` at the durable write, and everything else happens after that. The server
  bumps the generation, arms the lease, signs the wake snapshot, and POSTs it. The
  receiver verifies the Ed25519 signature against the cached JWKS, sends the
  email, and returns `{done:true}`, which auto-acks the tail and releases the
  lease in the same round trip. That is one signed POST, with no callback and no
  polling.

  A `503` schedules a retry with persisted backoff from 1 s to 60 s and 20%
  jitter, so the receipt is still sent after the receiver or the accepting origin
  recovers. A receiver that dedupes on `wake_id` keeps the email exactly-once.

  <Diagram
    name="seq-uc-webhook"
    label="Webhook: order receipts, with a retry"
    caption="The client gets its 204 (step 4) at the durable append, and the signed wake runs after it on its own schedule. A {done:true} receiver reduces delivery to one POST. A 503 enters the persisted backoff schedule and is re-fired after recovery."
  />

  ### An indexer worker pool <em>(pull)</em>

  A search index must track many `events/*` streams, and the work runs fully in
  parallel. So instead of one endpoint, a pool of identical stateless workers
  drains it. The pool registers one `pull-wake` subscription with `pattern:
  "events/*"` and a `wake_stream` the workers tail.

  A burst of appends coalesces into a single wake event the server writes to that
  stream. Every idle worker sees it and races to `claim`. Exactly one worker wins,
  and the rest get `409 ALREADY_CLAIMED` and go back to tailing. The winner reads
  the pending events up to the snapshot's tails, updates the index, and acks
  `{done:true}`. On a long batch it heartbeats. An ack *without* `done` extends
  the lease and keeps the claim.

  Elasticity is the point. Add pods and they start tailing. Kill a pod mid-batch
  and its lease expires, the recovery sweep re-arms the wake, and a surviving
  worker claims the *same* snapshot and finishes it. The re-claim reprocesses the
  same events, which is safe as long as the index write is idempotent, e.g. an
  upsert keyed by event offset. There is no leader and no rebalancing protocol.
  The lease ZSET and the fence are the whole coordination layer.

  <Diagram
    name="seq-uc-pullwake"
    label="Pull-wake: an elastic indexer pool"
    caption="Two workers race one wake event. The lease compare-and-set picks one and fences the other. When the winner dies mid-batch, the sweep re-arms and a survivor reclaims the same snapshot. This is at-least-once, made safe by an idempotent index write."
  />
</section>

<section class="block" id="surface">
  <SectionHead num="07" label="Surface" title="Every reserved <em>__ds</em> route" />

  The router reserves the whole `__ds` prefix before any stream handling. It
  returns 404 for unknown reserved paths rather than treating them as application
  streams. Errors use an `{"error":{"code":…}}` envelope.

  <Table wide>
    | Method | Path | Purpose | Success | Errors |
    | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
    | `PUT` | `__ds/subscriptions/:id` | create or re-confirm | `201` new · `200` re-confirm | `409 CONFIG_CONFLICT` · `400 WEBHOOK_URL_REJECTED` · `400 INVALID_REQUEST` |
    | `GET` | `__ds/subscriptions/:id` | read config + cursors | `200` | `404 NOT_FOUND` |
    | `DELETE` | `__ds/subscriptions/:id` | tombstone (idempotent) | `204` | none |
    | `POST` | `__ds/subscriptions/:id/streams` | add explicit links | `204` | `400 INVALID_REQUEST` |
    | `DELETE` | `__ds/subscriptions/:id/streams/:path` | remove explicit link | `204` | none |
    | `POST` | `__ds/subscriptions/:id/callback` | webhook async ack / heartbeat | `200` | `409 FENCED` · `401 TOKEN_INVALID` |
    | `POST` | `__ds/subscriptions/:id/claim` | pull-wake claim | `200` | `409 ALREADY_CLAIMED` · `404 NOT_FOUND` |
    | `POST` | `__ds/subscriptions/:id/ack` | pull-wake ack / heartbeat | `200` | `409 FENCED` · `401 TOKEN_INVALID` |
    | `POST` | `__ds/subscriptions/:id/release` | pull-wake release | `204` | `409 FENCED` · `401 TOKEN_INVALID` |
    | `GET` | `__ds/jwks.json` | webhook signing keys | `200` | none |
  </Table>

  <Table wide>
    | Method · Path | Time | Space | Dominated by |
    | --- | --- | --- | --- |
    | `PUT …/:id` (create) | `O(n)` | `O(n)` | n initial-link `HSET`s, and a glob also backfills matching streams |
    | `GET …/:id` | `O(P)` | `O(P)` | `HGETALL :links`, P cursors |
    | `DELETE …/:id` | `O(P + log L)` | frees `O(P)` | `DEL :links` + two schedule `ZREM`s + P fan-out `SREM`s |
    | `POST …/:id/streams` | `O(j)` | `O(j)` | one `HSET` per j added paths |
    | `DELETE …/streams/:path` | `O(1)` | `O(1)` | one `HSET`/`HDEL` |
    | `POST …/callback` · `…/ack` | `O(A + log L)` | `O(1)` | A cursor `HSET`s + one lease `ZADD`/`ZREM` |
    | `POST …/claim` | `O(log L)` | `O(1)` | the lease `ZADD` (the CAS) |
    | `POST …/release` | `O(log L)` | `O(1)` | two schedule `ZREM`s |
    | `GET …/jwks.json` | `O(1)` | `O(1)` | bounded `HGETALL :jwks` |
  </Table>

  The `ack` and `callback` endpoints are the same handler, fencing on
  `(generation, wake_id)` and serving as the heartbeat when called without `done`.
  The `claim` endpoint takes no callback token, because it is the lease-acquire
  step, while every other state-changing call carries a `Bearer` token bound to
  the subscription and generation.
</section>

<section class="block" id="tested">
  <SectionHead num="08" label="Tested" title="Killed mid-flight, <em>still delivered</em>" />

  The durability claim is checked by running the system, not only by argument.[^jepsen]
  A Jepsen-style harness runs Chronicle with two origins and Redis with AOF on a
  persistent volume and `appendfsync always`, all on a `k3d` cluster. It drives a
  known set of messages across many streams, injects faults with `kubectl delete
  pod --force`, and asserts one property. After the faults settle, every stream's
  `acked_offset` has reached its tail. Delivery is at-least-once, so the harness
  reports a duplicate factor but does not treat it as a failure.

  <Table>
    | Scenario | Fault | Result |
    | --- | --- | --- |
    | baseline | none | **6/6** streams at tail · 118 wakes / 120 msgs · dup ×1.00 |
    | origin-restart | kill one origin during the storm, then **all** origins after the final append | **12/12** at tail after both origins were replaced by fresh pods. In-memory state gone, every message re-delivered by the sweep · 895 wakes / 960 msgs |
    | redis-restart | delete Redis mid-storm, then it recreates and replays its AOF | **16/16** at tail after Redis came back · appends survived the outage via retry · 1681 wakes / 1920 msgs |
  </Table>

  The key run is **origin-restart**. Killing all origins after the last append
  destroys every in-memory wake those origins held, which is the state the
  reference engine keeps only in RAM. An in-memory implementation would leave
  those last messages durable in the log but never delivered. Chronicle's 12/12 is
  the sweep recovering them. The deliveries happen *after* the accepting origins no
  longer exist. Fewer wakes than messages is coalescing, where one wake's snapshot
  covers every pending offset up to the tail. It is not lost work.

  <Callout icon="info" label="Not yet tested">
    A true network partition between an origin and Redis, as opposed to a Redis
    restart. Also a partition that isolates one origin while the other sweeps. The
    fence makes both safe in principle. A partition nemesis is the next step.
  </Callout>
</section>

<section class="block" id="hardening">
  <SectionHead num="09" label="Hardening" title="Crash windows, <em>closed</em>" />

  Every durable write here is followed by a non-atomic step in Go, e.g. arm then
  emit, link then index, or commit then hook. A crash in that gap is the recurring
  source of a subscription bug. Four such windows are closed, each by the same
  idea. The durable record is the source of truth, and a background pass
  re-derives whatever the crash dropped. None of these changes the at-least-once
  guarantee or the fence.

  <Table wide>
    | Window | Symptom after a crash | Fix |
    | --- | --- | --- |
    | **Expired-lease takeover** | a second worker claimed a lease whose deadline had passed and *reused* the old `generation`/`wake_id`, so the deposed worker could still ack. Two holders shared one fence | the claim now rotates the fence on takeover, minting a fresh `generation` and `wake_id`, so the deposed worker's late ack is `FENCED` |
    | **Pull-wake arm-then-emit** | a pull-wake armed (`phase=waking`) but crashed before writing its wake event, then stayed stranded forever. The sweep only re-woke idle subs and a later append coalesced | `arm_wake` marks the wake unsent, and the recovery sweep re-emits any pull-wake still `waking` and unsent. Duplicate wake events are fence-safe |
    | **Missed glob link** | a stream created during an outage never ran `OnStreamCreated`, so a matching pattern subscription never linked it, and it does not recover on its own | a separate slow reconcile loop lists streams and re-links missing glob matches, at the beginning offset if the stream is newer than the subscription, at the tail if older |
    | **Dropped fan-out index** | the canonical link survived but its fan-out index entry did not, so appends fell back to sweep latency | the reconcile loop rebuilds the index from the canonical links. It mirrors links and never invents membership |
  </Table>

  Only the first window touched correctness. The other three are liveness
  problems, a stranded wake, a missing link, and a slow trigger, all recovered by
  the durable record plus a sweep.

  <Callout icon="info" label="Sweep, not outbox">
    The pull-wake recovery re-emits from the existing recovery sweep rather than a
    dedicated delivery outbox with its own schedule queue and worker. The sweep is
    already the recovery path for every other stuck wake. A one-field marker for
    whether the wake was emitted folds the pull-wake case into the scan the sweep
    already runs. That is the same at-least-once guarantee with far less code. The
    cost is recovery at sweep latency, which is the right trade for a post-crash
    event.
  </Callout>
</section>

<section class="block" id="scaling">
  <SectionHead num="10" label="Scaling" title="The sweep reads in <em>one batch</em>" />

  The recovery sweep is the durability backstop from section 05. On each tick it
  re-derives pending work for *every* subscription by reading its durable cursor
  and the tail of each linked stream. That read pattern is where the poll meets
  its ceiling, and where a small change moves the ceiling by two orders of
  magnitude.

  The naive sweep issued those reads one Redis round trip at a time, with one
  `HGETALL` per subscription and then one `HGET` per linked stream inside the
  loop. With `K` subscriptions and `P` links each, that is `1 + K + K·P`
  *sequential* round trips. Round-trip latency dominates, so a tick costs about
  `K·(1 + P) × RTT`. At a real round-trip time near 0.5 ms, a single tick stops
  fitting its sweep-interval budget at only a few thousand subscriptions.

  The fix keeps the same decision and changes only how the reads are issued, by
  pipelining them. The subscription reads go out in chunked batches, every linked
  tail is read in one batched pass, and the pending-work test runs in memory
  against the tails fetched up front.

  <Table wide>
    | Per sweep tick | Naive (per-link) | Batched (pipelined) |
    | --- | --- | --- |
    | Subscription reads | `K` round trips | `⌈K / 512⌉` round trips |
    | Tail reads | `K·P` round trips | `⌈U / 512⌉` round trips |
    | Total round trips | `1 + K + K·P` | `≈ 1 + K/512 + U/512` |
    | Wall-time | `≈ K·(1 + P) × RTT`, RTT-bound | a few batches, throughput-bound |
    | Pending test | `P` reads per sub | in memory, short-circuits |
  </Table>

  Throughout: `K` = subscriptions, `P` = links per subscription, `U` = unique
  linked streams across the swept set, `512` = the pipeline chunk size.

  ```text title="sweepOnce: batched reads, then an in-memory decision"
  sweepOnce():
    ids   = SMEMBERS subs                 # 1 round trip
    subs  = GetMany(ids)                  # ⌈K/512⌉ pipelined round trips
    paths = distinct paths over subs.links
    tails = TailOffsets(paths)            # ⌈U/512⌉ pipelined round trips
    for sub in subs:                      # no Redis in this loop
        if pull_wake(sub) and stranded(sub): reemit(sub); continue
        if lease_expired(sub):               expire(sub)
        if idle(sub) and pending(sub.links, tails): issueWake(sub)

  pending(links, tails):                  # short-circuits on the first hit
    for l in links:
        if tails[l.path] > l.acked_offset: return true
    return false
  ```

  <Callout icon="info" label="Same decision, fewer trips">
    The batched sweep issues the *same* wakes as the per-link one, with the same
    idle-and-pending test, stranded-pull-wake re-emit, and lease expiry. Only the
    read schedule changed, to a handful of pipelined batches instead of one round
    trip per subscription and per link.
  </Callout>

  ### What it buys, <em>measured</em>

  A `BenchmarkSweepOnce` seeds `K` idle subscriptions with `P` links each against
  a local Redis and times one sweep. The per-link baseline runs into seconds past
  a few thousand subscriptions. The batched sweep stays in the low milliseconds.

  <Table>
    | Subscriptions × links | Per-link sweep | Batched sweep | Speed-up |
    | --- | --- | --- | --- |
    | 1,000 × 1 | 1.09 s | 12.5 ms | ~87× |
    | 1,000 × 5 | 3.16 s | 29.7 ms | ~107× |
    | 10,000 × 1 | 11.3 s | 122 ms | ~93× |
    | 5,000 × 5 | 48.4 s | 137 ms | ~354× |
  </Table>

  At 10,000 subscriptions and 5 links each, the per-link sweep is
  `1 + 10,000 + 50,000 ≈ 60,001` sequential round trips against the batched
  sweep's `1 + ⌈10,000/512⌉ + ⌈50,000/512⌉ = 119`. The answer is the same. The
  difference is whether recovery keeps up.

  <Complexity
    label="Complexity: round trips, not big-O, are the lever"
    time="O(K + U) work · ⌈K/512⌉ + ⌈U/512⌉ round trips per tick"
    space="O(K + U) for the swept ids and the tail map"
    why="The big-O work is unchanged. The sweep still touches every subscription and every distinct linked tail. What changed is the round-trip count. Pipelining reduces 1 + K + K·P sequential trips to a chunked handful, so a tick is bounded by Redis throughput rather than by K times round-trip latency. An optional per-tick cap (<code>-sweep-batch</code>) also bounds the work itself for very large keyspaces, by rolling a cursor across ticks so every subscription is still covered."
  />
</section>

<section class="block" id="open">
  <SectionHead num="11" label="Open questions" title="What the dual write leaves <em>open</em>" />

  Everything above rests on one rule. The wake carries no authority. The durable
  cursor and the stream tail are the truth, and the wake is a derived signal a
  background pass can recompute. This is what makes the append-then-notify *dual
  write* safe to lose. Drop the second write and the sweep re-derives the same
  answer from durable state. It also marks the design's open edges, and all of
  them are about the latency and cost of that recompute, never its correctness.

  - **No recovery faster than the sweep.** A wake lost to a crash is re-derived
    by event-triggered recovery, with the 30 s sweep floor as the backstop. A glob
    link missed during an outage waits for the next reconcile, about 30 s.
    Recovery is a post-crash event, so that latency is the right cost to pay. A
    loss-sensitive pull-wake workload has no faster path than the sweep today. The
    named upgrade is the delivery [outbox
    sketched in
    `docs/research/10`](https://github.com/adityavkk/chronicle/blob/main/docs/research/10-subscription-hardening-handoff.md),
    a due-scored wake schedule that fires before the next tick, at the cost of a
    third schedule ZSET and a fourth worker loop.

  - **The fast path is in-process, not cross-origin.** The append-time hook
    (`OnStreamAppend`) is a synchronous Go call on the origin that handled the
    append. It is not the `ds:notify` pub/sub the direct-consumer long-poll uses.
    So an append on origin A wakes A's subscribers at append latency, while a
    subscriber a peer origin would serve waits for that peer's sweep. Redis holds
    the durable state, so any origin *can* act. The open question is whether the
    fast path should reach across origins rather than leaving the sweep to bridge
    them.

  - **The poll still has a ceiling, just a higher one.** Batching the sweep's
    reads in section 10 moved the cliff from a few thousand subscriptions to well
    past 10k on one replica, but two edges stay open. The sweep still runs
    redundantly on every replica, `O(N·K)` against the shared `{__ds}` slot, and
    it is still `O(K)` work per tick. Past what batching buys, the fixes are
    sharding the sweep across replicas, where the fence already makes a missharded
    item harmless, or an event-driven backstop, the doc-10 outbox, that touches
    only subscriptions with owed work instead of scanning all K.

  None of these is a correctness gap. The fence and the forward-only cursor hold
  no matter which wake is lost or doubled.
</section>
