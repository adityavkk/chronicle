<Tldr>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>A subscription is a durable cursor per stream.</strong> When any linked stream's tail advances past its <code>acked_offset</code>, the subscription has pending work and the server wakes a worker.</li>
    <li><strong>Two delivery modes, one model.</strong> <code>webhook</code> signs and POSTs a notification to your URL; <code>pull-wake</code> writes a wake event to a stream your workers poll and claim. Both share the same cursor, generation, lease, and stream-membership.</li>
    <li><strong>The generation fence keeps duplicates safe.</strong> Every wake carries a monotonic <code>generation</code> and a single-use <code>wake_id</code>; a stale callback is <code>409 FENCED</code>. Delivery is at-least-once, and the fence makes a duplicate harmless.</li>
    <li><strong>It is all in Redis, under one hash tag.</strong> The reference engines keep cursors, leases, and retry schedules in an in-memory map that a restart loses. Chronicle persists them under <code>{"{__ds}"}</code> so they survive — and adds a recovery sweep, because a durable cursor alone is never re-checked for an idle subscription after a crash.</li>
    <li><strong>Verified under fault injection.</strong> A Jepsen-style suite kills origins and Redis mid-flight on Kubernetes; every durably-appended message still reaches its cursor.</li>
  </ul>
</Tldr>

<section class="block" id="model">
  <SectionHead num="01" label="Model" title="Durable cursors that <em>wake workers</em>" />

  A subscription links one or more streams and tracks an `acked_offset` for each
  — the last offset a worker has processed, inclusive.[^protocol] When an append
  pushes a linked stream's tail past its cursor, the subscription has *pending
  work*, and the server issues a **wake**: a unit of delivery carrying a snapshot
  of which streams are behind and how far. A worker processes the events and acks
  the new offsets; the cursor advances; the subscription goes idle until the next
  append. Nothing replays history by default — a new link starts at the stream's
  current tail.

  A subscription delivers wakes one of two ways, chosen at creation by `type`:

  <Table>
    | | `webhook` | `pull-wake` |
    | --- | --- | --- |
    | Who initiates delivery | the **server** POSTs to your `webhook.url` | your **workers** poll a `wake_stream` and claim |
    | Wake carrier | a signed HTTP notification | a `{type:"wake"}` event appended to a durable stream |
    | When the lease arms | at wake issue | at claim |
    | Auth on the work | the server signs (Ed25519); you verify | a service JWT to claim; a claim token to ack |
    | Best for | push to a known endpoint behind TLS | a worker pool draining at its own pace |
  </Table>

  Both modes share the same machinery: the per-stream cursor, a monotonic
  `generation` counter, a `wake_id` per wake, a lease that bounds worker
  liveness, and the glob/explicit stream-membership model. The difference is only
  who carries the wake and when the lease starts.

  <Callout icon="info" label="The durability gap this closes">
    The protocol calls subscriptions *durable cursors* and requires their state
    — the cursor, the retry schedule, the lease — to survive a server
    restart.[^research] Both reference implementations (the TypeScript dev server
    and the Go Caddy plugin) keep all of it in an in-memory map and lose it on
    restart. That is the gap Chronicle exists to close: the log already persists
    to Redis; the subscription layer persists there too, and a sweep
    re-evaluates what is owed.
  </Callout>
</section>

<section class="block" id="webhook">
  <SectionHead num="02" label="Webhook" title="Sign, POST, <em>auto-ack</em>" />

  Create a webhook subscription with a `PUT`. The `pattern` is a glob over
  stream-root-relative paths (`*` is one segment, `**` is zero or more);
  matching streams are linked at their current tail. The response echoes the
  signing metadata but never a shared secret — deliveries are signed
  asymmetrically.

  ```http title="Register a webhook subscription"
  PUT /v1/stream/__ds/subscriptions/sub-1 HTTP/1.1
  Content-Type: application/json

  {"type":"webhook","pattern":"events/*",
   "webhook":{"url":"https://worker.example/hooks"},
   "lease_ttl_ms":30000}

  HTTP/1.1 201 Created
  Content-Type: application/json

  {"id":"sub-1","type":"webhook","status":"active",
   "streams":[],
   "webhook":{"url":"https://worker.example/hooks",
     "signing":{"alg":"ed25519","kid":"ds_abc123",
       "jwks_url":"https://server.example/v1/stream/__ds/jwks.json"}}}
  ```

  Webhook URLs are validated against SSRF: production targets must be `https`,
  and RFC1918, link-local, loopback-by-DNS, and cloud-metadata addresses are
  rejected with `400 WEBHOOK_URL_REJECTED`.[^ssrf] A literal loopback host is the
  development exception; a trusted-network flag accepts private targets for
  in-cluster receivers.

  When a matching append lands and the subscription is idle, the server bumps the
  `generation`, mints a `wake_id`, arms the lease, and POSTs a signed
  notification. The `Webhook-Signature` is an Ed25519 signature over
  `<timestamp>.<raw_body>`; the `kid` selects the key from the JWKS, fetched once
  and cached.

  ```http title="The wake (server → your receiver)"
  POST https://worker.example/hooks HTTP/1.1
  Content-Type: application/json
  Webhook-Signature: t=1778324210,kid=ds_abc123,ed25519=<base64url>

  {"subscription_id":"sub-1","wake_id":"w_abc123","generation":7,
   "streams":[{"path":"events/abc","link_type":"glob",
     "acked_offset":"...0042","tail_offset":"...0084","has_pending":true}],
   "callback_url":"https://server.example/v1/stream/__ds/subscriptions/sub-1/callback",
   "callback_token":"eyJ..."}
  ```

  Your receiver can finish two ways. Return `{done:true}` and the server
  **auto-acks** the tails in that snapshot and releases the lease — the whole
  round trip is one POST. Or return `200` and process asynchronously, then call
  back to `callback_url` with the offsets you reached. A non-2xx or a transport
  error schedules a retry with exponential backoff from 1 s to 60 s and 20%
  jitter; the retry schedule is persisted, so a restart honors it.

<section class="block" id="fence">
  <SectionHead num="03" label="Fence" title="Generation is the <em>fence</em>, not the lease" />

  A subscription moves through three phases: **idle** (no wake in flight, no lease
  held), **waking** (a wake issued but not yet claimed or completed), and **live**
  (a worker holds the lease, extended by heartbeats). The lease bounds how long a
  worker may hold a wake; when it expires the server clears the holder and, if
  work remains, issues a fresh wake.

  Correctness, though, does not rest on the lease. A time-based lease cannot fence
  a worker that has merely stalled, so the server treats delivery as
  **at-least-once** and leans on a monotonic token instead.[^fence] Every
  callback, ack, and release is rejected `409 FENCED` unless all of these match
  current state:

  <Table>
    | Check | Must equal |
    | --- | --- |
    | Bearer token | a valid token for this subscription |
    | token `generation` | the current generation |
    | request `generation` | the current generation |
    | request `wake_id` | the current `wake_id` (non-empty) |
  </Table>

  Because the fence is exact, a duplicate or late callback cannot advance the
  cursor twice — it loses the comparison and is rejected. That is what makes
  at-least-once safe: a wake may be delivered more than once (a lease reclaim, a
  retry, a sweep), and the second delivery is harmless.

  ```http title="Ack a wake, then watch a replay get fenced"
  POST /v1/stream/__ds/subscriptions/sub-1/callback HTTP/1.1
  Authorization: Bearer eyJ...
  Content-Type: application/json

  {"wake_id":"w_abc123","generation":7,
   "acks":[{"stream":"events/abc","offset":"...0084"}],"done":true}

  HTTP/1.1 200 OK
  {"ok":true,"next_wake":false}

  ← a replay of the same body, after the wake was cleared:
  HTTP/1.1 409 Conflict
  {"error":{"code":"FENCED"}}
  ```

<section class="block" id="pull-wake">
  <SectionHead num="04" label="Pull-wake" title="Claim a <em>subscription-scoped lease</em>" />

  A pull-wake subscription names a `wake_stream` — an ordinary durable stream the
  application creates. Instead of POSTing, the server appends a small wake event
  to that stream; a pool of workers tails it and races to **claim** the
  subscription. The claim is a compare-and-set on the lease: the first worker
  wins and gets a snapshot plus a claim token; a second worker, while the lease
  is held, gets `409 ALREADY_CLAIMED` naming the current holder.

  ```http title="Claim, then ack"
  POST /v1/stream/__ds/subscriptions/sub-1/claim HTTP/1.1
  Content-Type: application/json

  {"worker":"worker-1"}

  HTTP/1.1 200 OK
  {"wake_id":"w_abc123","generation":7,"token":"eyJ...","lease_ttl_ms":30000,
   "streams":[{"path":"events/abc","link_type":"glob",
     "acked_offset":"...0042","tail_offset":"...0084","has_pending":true}]}

  ← a second worker, while the lease is held:
  HTTP/1.1 409 Conflict
  {"error":{"code":"ALREADY_CLAIMED","current_holder":"worker-1","generation":7}}
  ```

  The worker acks through the same fenced endpoint as a webhook callback — with
  `done:true` to finish, or without it to heartbeat and keep the lease. A worker
  can also `release` without acking; if work remains, the server writes another
  wake event for the next claim.

<section class="block" id="durability">
  <SectionHead num="05" label="Durability" title="Why a cursor <em>isn't enough</em>" />

  The whole control plane lives in Redis under a single fixed hash tag,
  `{__ds}`, so every correctness-critical script touches one slot and is
  cluster-safe. The high-cardinality per-stream log stays sharded under each
  stream's own tag; only the control plane — far lower cardinality, like a
  coordinator — is centralized.

  <Table>
    | Key | Type | Holds |
    | --- | --- | --- |
    | `ds:{__ds}:sub:<id>` | hash | config + runtime: `generation`, `wake_id`, `phase`, `holder`, `lease_until_ns`, retry state |
    | `ds:{__ds}:sub:<id>:links` | hash | the durable cursors — `path → "<link_type>:<acked_offset>"` |
    | `ds:{__ds}:subs` | set | every subscription id, for the sweep |
    | `ds:{__ds}:stream:<path>` | set | fan-out index: which subscriptions a stream wakes |
    | `ds:{__ds}:sched:lease` | sorted set | `sub → lease_expiry_ns` — the liveness lease, as a due-scored timer |
    | `ds:{__ds}:sched:retry` | sorted set | `sub → next_attempt_ns` — the webhook backoff schedule |
    | `ds:{__ds}:jwks` | hash | Ed25519 signing keys, so the `kid` is stable across restarts |
  </Table>

  ### What the control plane <em>costs</em>

  The control plane is deliberately *low-cardinality* — a coordinator's worth
  of state, not a log's — which is what makes centralizing it under one slot
  affordable. The structures and their costs:

  - **The `sub:<id>` and `:links` hashes** are `O(1)` per field; reading a
    subscription's full cursor set is `O(P)` for its P linked streams.
  - **The `subs` and `stream:<path>` sets** are `O(1)` to add or remove.
    `SMEMBERS` is `O(K)` over all K subscriptions, or `O(F)` over the F
    subscriptions one stream wakes.
  - **The `sched:lease` and `sched:retry` sorted sets** are the timers: an arm
    or release is one `ZADD`/`ZREM`, `O(log L)` for L scheduled entries; a
    sweep of what is due is `ZRANGEBYSCORE`, `O(log L + D)` for D due.[^cost]

  So every per-request operation is `O(1)` hash work plus at most one
  `O(log L)` schedule touch. The one `O(K)` operation is the periodic sweep —
  and K is the *subscription* count, not the message count. That is the whole
  reason the control plane is kept separate from the high-cardinality
  per-stream log: a coordinator's tables stay small even when the log does not.

  ```text title="The schedule is a due-scored ZSET — a timer you can crash with"
  ZADD :sched:lease (now + ttl) sub           # arm a lease        O(log L)
  due = ZRANGEBYSCORE :sched:lease -inf now    # what is overdue?   O(log L + D)
  for sub in due:
      ZADD :sched:lease (now + visibility) sub  # re-score forward   O(log L)
  # claim-by-re-score, never ZREM: a worker that dies leaves the item to
  # fall due again. L = scheduled subscriptions, D = due this tick.
  ```

  ```text title="Wake fan-out is a SET read; the sweep is the one O(K) walk"
  SMEMBERS :stream:<path>             # the F subs this append wakes   O(F)
  for sub in those: arm_wake(sub)     # each O(log L)                  => O(F log L)

  for id in SMEMBERS :subs:           # every subscription, each tick  O(K)
      expire_lease(id); if behind: arm_wake(id)   # each O(log L)
  # => O(K log L) per sweep — linear in subscription count, not log size.
  ```

  Throughout: **K** = subscriptions, **P** = streams linked to one
  subscription, **F** = subscriptions a stream wakes, **L** = entries in a
  schedule ZSET, **D** = entries due in a tick, **A** = streams acked in one
  request.

  Persisting the cursor is necessary but **not sufficient**. Wake creation is
  purely event-driven: a wake is minted only by a new append or by an in-flight
  wake's lifecycle ending. After a restart, an *idle* subscription whose streams
  are quiet is never re-evaluated — its durable cursor now makes the answer
  computable, but nothing asks the question.[^research] So Chronicle runs a
  **recovery sweep** on boot and on an interval: it walks every subscription,
  expires stale leases, and for any idle subscription with pending work, re-issues
  the wake the crash dropped. The append-time hook is the fast path; the sweep is
  the backstop.

  **re-scores** due members forward rather than removing them — so a worker that
  dies after taking a due item leaves it to fall due again and be reclaimed. That
  is at-least-once by construction, with no leader election: single-threaded Redis
  makes exactly one origin's re-score win per tick.

  <Callout icon="alert" label="Durability honesty">
    The guarantee is at-least-once, fenced — not exactly-once. The loss window is
    set by the fsync policy: `appendfsync everysec` can lose ~1–2 s of writes on a
    hard crash, which would surface as a stream stuck below its tail. Honoring the
    protocol's MUSTs means `appendfsync always` (and `WAITAOF` to a replica). Even
    then a failover can drop the last unsynced write. That is exactly why the
    design leans on the fence rather than on never losing one.
  </Callout>

<section class="block" id="use-cases">
  <SectionHead num="06" label="End to end" title="Two flows, <em>start to finish</em>" />

  The machinery above composes into the two shapes you actually deploy: a
  **push** to a service that owns an endpoint, and a **pull** by a pool that
  drains at its own pace. Both run on the same cursor, generation, lease, and
  fence — only the carrier and the moment the lease arms differ.

  ### A webhook receipt service <em>(push)</em>

  A notification service has one job: email a receipt when an order is paid or
  shipped. It registers a single webhook subscription with `pattern:
  "orders/**"` and its receiver URL — every order stream it will ever care
  about, including ones created later, is matched at its tail. From then on it
  is passive; the server initiates.

  When the order service appends `paid` to `orders/A1234`, that append earns
  its `204` at the durable write, and everything else happens off that clock.
  The server bumps the generation, arms the lease, signs the wake snapshot, and
  POSTs it. The receiver verifies the Ed25519 signature against the cached
  JWKS, sends the email, and returns `{done:true}` — which auto-acks the tail
  and releases the lease in the same round trip. One signed POST: no callback,
  no polling.

  The load-bearing part is the unhappy path. A `503` schedules a retry with
  jittered backoff (1 s to 60 s), and the schedule is *persisted*, so the
  receipt is still sent after the receiver — or the accepting origin —
  recovers. Delivery is at-least-once: if a `200` is lost and the wake is
  retried, the receiver can run twice. The fence keeps the *cursor* correct; a
  receiver that dedupes on `wake_id` keeps the *email* exactly-once.

  <Diagram
    name="seq-uc-webhook"
    label="Webhook — order receipts, with a retry"
    caption="The 204 (step 4) is earned at the durable append; the signed wake rides a different clock. A {done:true} receiver collapses delivery to one POST; a 503 falls into the persisted backoff schedule and is re-fired after recovery."
  />

  ### An indexer worker pool <em>(pull)</em>

  A search index must track a firehose of `events/*` streams, and the work is
  embarrassingly parallel — so instead of one endpoint, a pool of identical
  stateless workers drains it. The pool registers one `pull-wake` subscription
  with `pattern: "events/*"` and a `wake_stream` the workers tail.

  A burst of appends coalesces into a single wake event the server writes to
  that stream; every idle worker sees it and races to `claim`. The claim is a
  compare-and-set on the lease — single-threaded Redis lets exactly one worker
  win, and the rest get `409 ALREADY_CLAIMED` and go back to tailing. The
  winner reads the pending events up to the snapshot's tails, updates the
  index, and acks `{done:true}`. On a long batch it heartbeats — an ack
  *without* `done` extends the lease and keeps the claim.

  Elasticity is the point. Add pods and they just start tailing. Kill a pod
  mid-batch and its lease expires; the recovery sweep re-arms the wake, and a
  surviving worker claims the *same* snapshot and finishes it. The re-claim
  reprocessing the same events is safe as long as the index write is idempotent
  (an upsert keyed by event offset). No leader, no rebalancing protocol — the
  lease ZSET and the fence are the whole coordination layer.

  <Diagram
    name="seq-uc-pullwake"
    label="Pull-wake — an elastic indexer pool"
    caption="Two workers race one wake event; the lease CAS picks one and fences the other. When the winner dies mid-batch, the sweep re-arms and a survivor reclaims the same snapshot — at-least-once, made safe by an idempotent index write."
  />
</section>

<section class="block" id="surface">
  <SectionHead num="07" label="Surface" title="Every reserved <em>__ds</em> route" />

  The router reserves the whole `__ds` prefix ahead of stream handling and 404s
  unknown reserved paths rather than treating them as application streams. Errors
  use an `{"error":{"code":…}}` envelope.

  <Table wide>
    | Method | Path | Purpose | Success | Errors |
    | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
    | `PUT` | `__ds/subscriptions/:id` | create or re-confirm | `201` new · `200` re-confirm | `409 CONFIG_CONFLICT` · `400 WEBHOOK_URL_REJECTED` · `400 INVALID_REQUEST` |
    | `GET` | `__ds/subscriptions/:id` | read config + cursors | `200` | `404 NOT_FOUND` |
    | `DELETE` | `__ds/subscriptions/:id` | tombstone (idempotent) | `204` | — |
    | `POST` | `__ds/subscriptions/:id/streams` | add explicit links | `204` | `400 INVALID_REQUEST` |
    | `DELETE` | `__ds/subscriptions/:id/streams/:path` | remove explicit link | `204` | — |
    | `POST` | `__ds/subscriptions/:id/callback` | webhook async ack / heartbeat | `200` | `409 FENCED` · `401 TOKEN_INVALID` |
    | `POST` | `__ds/subscriptions/:id/claim` | pull-wake claim | `200` | `409 ALREADY_CLAIMED` · `404 NOT_FOUND` |
    | `POST` | `__ds/subscriptions/:id/ack` | pull-wake ack / heartbeat | `200` | `409 FENCED` · `401 TOKEN_INVALID` |
    | `POST` | `__ds/subscriptions/:id/release` | pull-wake release | `204` | `409 FENCED` · `401 TOKEN_INVALID` |
    | `GET` | `__ds/jwks.json` | webhook signing keys | `200` | — |
  </Table>

  Every route's cost falls out of the same structures: `O(1)` hash work, an
  `O(log L)` schedule touch where a lease moves, and a per-request fan-out only
  where a request names many paths.

  <Table wide>
    | Method · Path | Time | Space | Dominated by |
    | --- | --- | --- | --- |
    | `PUT …/:id` (create) | `O(n)` | `O(n)` | n initial-link `HSET`s; a glob also backfills matching streams |
    | `GET …/:id` | `O(P)` | `O(P)` | `HGETALL :links` — P cursors |
    | `DELETE …/:id` | `O(P + log L)` | frees `O(P)` | `DEL :links` + two schedule `ZREM`s + P fan-out `SREM`s |
    | `POST …/:id/streams` | `O(j)` | `O(j)` | one `HSET` per j added paths |
    | `DELETE …/streams/:path` | `O(1)` | `O(1)` | one `HSET`/`HDEL` |
    | `POST …/callback` · `…/ack` | `O(A + log L)` | `O(1)` | A cursor `HSET`s + one lease `ZADD`/`ZREM` |
    | `POST …/claim` | `O(log L)` | `O(1)` | the lease `ZADD` (the CAS) |
    | `POST …/release` | `O(log L)` | `O(1)` | two schedule `ZREM`s |
    | `GET …/jwks.json` | `O(1)` | `O(1)` | bounded `HGETALL :jwks` |
  </Table>

  The `ack` and `callback` endpoints are the same handler — both fence on
  `(generation, wake_id)` and both double as the heartbeat when called without
  `done`. The `claim` endpoint takes no callback token (it is the lease-acquire
  step); every other state-changing call carries a `Bearer` token bound to the
  subscription and generation.
</section>

<section class="block" id="tested">
  <SectionHead num="08" label="Tested" title="Killed mid-flight, <em>still delivered</em>" />

  The durability claim is checked empirically, not just argued.[^jepsen] A
  Jepsen-style harness runs Chronicle (two origins) and Redis (AOF on a
  persistent volume, `appendfsync always`) on a `k3d` cluster, drives a known set
  of messages across many streams, injects faults with `kubectl delete pod
  --force`, and asserts one property: after the faults settle, every stream's
  `acked_offset` has reached its tail. Delivery is at-least-once, so a duplicate
  factor is reported but is not a failure.

  <Table>
    | Scenario | Fault | Result |
    | --- | --- | --- |
    | baseline | none | **6/6** streams at tail · 118 wakes / 120 msgs · dup ×1.00 |
    | origin-restart | kill one origin during the storm, then **all** origins after the final append | **12/12** at tail after both origins were replaced by fresh pods — in-memory state gone, every message re-delivered by the sweep · 895 wakes / 960 msgs |
    | redis-restart | delete Redis mid-storm; it recreates and replays its AOF | **16/16** at tail after Redis came back · appends rode the outage via retry · 1681 wakes / 1920 msgs |
  </Table>

  The load-bearing run is **origin-restart**: killing all origins after the last
  append destroys every in-memory wake those origins held — exactly the state the
  reference engine keeps only in RAM. An in-memory implementation would leave
  those last messages durable in the log but never delivered. Chronicle's 12/12 is
  the sweep recovering them: deliveries observed *after* the accepting origins no
  longer exist. Fewer wakes than messages is coalescing: one wake's snapshot
  covers every pending offset up to the tail. It is not lost work.

  <Callout icon="info" label="Not yet tested">
    A true network partition between an origin and Redis (as opposed to a Redis
    restart), and a partition that isolates one origin while the other sweeps. The
    fence makes both safe in principle; a partition nemesis is the next step.
  </Callout>
</section>

<section class="block" id="hardening">
  <SectionHead num="09" label="Hardening" title="Crash windows, <em>closed</em>" />

  Every durable write here is followed by a non-atomic step in Go — arm then
  emit, link then index, commit then hook. A crash in that gap is the recurring
  shape of a subscription bug. Four such windows are closed, each behind one idea:
  the durable record is the source of truth, and a background pass re-derives
  whatever the crash dropped. None changes the at-least-once guarantee or the
  fence.

  <Table wide>
    | Window | Symptom after a crash | Fix |
    | --- | --- | --- |
    | **Expired-lease takeover** | a second worker claimed a lease whose deadline had passed and *reused* the old `generation`/`wake_id`, so the deposed worker could still ack — two holders of one fence | the claim now rotates the fence (mints a fresh `generation` and `wake_id`) on takeover, so the deposed worker's late ack is `FENCED` |
    | **Pull-wake arm-then-emit** | a pull-wake armed (`phase=waking`) but crashed before writing its wake event stayed stranded forever — the sweep only re-woke idle subs and a later append coalesced | `arm_wake` marks the wake unsent; the recovery sweep re-emits any pull-wake still `waking` and unsent. Duplicate wake events are fence-safe |
    | **Missed glob link** | a stream created during an outage never ran `OnStreamCreated`, so a matching pattern subscription never linked it — and it does not self-heal | a separate slow reconcile loop lists streams and re-links missing glob matches — at the beginning offset if the stream is newer than the subscription, the tail if older |
    | **Dropped fan-out index** | the canonical link survived but its fan-out index entry did not, so appends fell back to sweep latency | the reconcile loop rebuilds the index from the canonical links; it mirrors links and never invents membership |
  </Table>

  Only the first touched correctness: reusing a fence across a lease takeover let
  two workers act on one wake. Forward-only acks kept that from corrupting a
  cursor, but the rotation restores the single-holder invariant the fence exists
  to provide. The other three are liveness — a stranded wake, a missing link, a
  slow trigger — recovered by the durable record plus a sweep.

  <Callout icon="info" label="Sweep, not outbox">
    The pull-wake recovery re-emits from the existing recovery sweep rather than a
    dedicated delivery outbox with its own schedule queue and worker. The sweep is
    already the recovery path for every other stuck wake, so a one-field "was this
    emitted?" marker folds the pull-wake case into the scan the sweep already runs
    — same at-least-once guarantee, far less machinery. The cost is recovery at
    sweep latency, the right trade for a post-crash event.
  </Callout>
</section>

<section class="block" id="open">
  <SectionHead num="10" label="Open questions" title="What the dual write leaves <em>open</em>" />

  Everything above rests on one move: the wake carries no authority. The durable
  cursor and the stream tail are the truth; the wake is a derived signal a
  background pass can recompute. That is what makes the append-then-notify *dual
  write* safe to lose — drop the second write and the sweep re-derives the same
  answer from durable state. It also draws the design's open edges, and all of
  them are about the latency and cost of that recompute, never its correctness.

  - **No recovery faster than the sweep.** A wake lost to a crash is re-derived
    at the next sweep tick (~2 s); a glob link missed during an outage waits for
    the next reconcile (~30 s). Recovery is a post-crash event, so that latency is
    the right cost to pay — but a loss-sensitive pull-wake workload has no
    sub-sweep path today. The named upgrade is the delivery [outbox sketched in
    `docs/research/10`](https://github.com/adityavkk/chronicle/blob/main/docs/research/10-subscription-hardening-handoff.md):
    a due-scored wake schedule that fires before the next tick, at the cost of a
    third schedule ZSET and a fourth worker loop.

  - **The fast path is in-process, not cross-origin.** The append-time hook
    (`OnStreamAppend`) is a synchronous Go call on the origin that handled the
    append — not the `ds:notify` pub/sub the direct-consumer long-poll rides. So
    an append on origin A wakes A's subscribers at append latency, while a
    subscriber a peer origin would serve waits for that peer's sweep. Redis holds
    the durable state, so any origin *can* act; the open question is whether the
    fast path should reach across origins rather than leaving the sweep to bridge
    them.

  - **The backstop is a poll, and a poll has a ceiling.** The sweep is `O(K)`
    every couple of seconds (K subscriptions) and the reconcile is
    `O(pattern-subs × streams)` every 30 s. Both are bounded to coordinator
    cardinality by design, but a large enough K or stream count turns the
    reconverge into a keyspace-sized scan on a fixed clock. An event-driven or
    sharded backstop is the scaling question the current poll defers.

  None of these is a correctness gap: the fence and the forward-only cursor hold
  no matter which wake is lost or doubled. They are the standing costs of
  preferring a best-effort hint plus a poll over a durable delivery queue — each
  with a named upgrade if a workload ever forces the trade.
</section>
