<Callout icon="alert" label="What this proves, and what it does not">
  The slot-homed, work-sharded design is built and green locally. PR #18 landed
  the whole horizontal-scale epic on main: subscriptions are slot-homed into 256
  `{__ds:h}` slots, ownership is leased over a `{ownership}` keyspace, and the
  claim is split per `(subId, g)`. Its safety and liveness suite passes on a real
  Chronicle plus Redis stack: T1 to T5 GREEN, L1 and L3 GREEN, and the contention
  suite C1 to C3 with doc-05 gate #6 GREEN. The cloud rig deployed that same
  post-epic binary, so the numbers on this page measure the sharded design, not a
  pre-sharding baseline. The one thing the cloud rig could not exercise is the
  multi-shard layout: it ran on a single-node Memorystore BASIC, which has one
  hash-slot range on one node, so the slots never spanned nodes. The measured
  gates therefore show the sharded binary on a single-slot substrate no longer
  fence-storms as replicas grow, holds its sweep at 10,000 subscriptions, and
  keeps its claim lease linearizable under contention. They do **not** yet carry
  the cross-node, multi-shard fan-out number, and they do not re-prove the 12
  replica point. Those two gates, plus the L2, L4, and L5 multi-node liveness
  runs, stayed unmeasured on cloud because GCP project quota blocked the 3-shard
  Redis Cluster and the larger node pool. See [the honest limits](#limits) for
  exactly which, and why.
</Callout>

<section class="block" id="goal">
  <SectionHead num="01" label="The goal" title="What we set out to <em>prove</em>" />

  Chronicle is one Go binary that serves durable streams and runs the
  subscription control plane. The open question was whether adding replicas
  helps the control plane or hurts it. A prior load test, the agents runtime
  driven against chronicle as its Durable-Streams backend, had answered "hurts"
  in the worst way.

  At 6 replicas the wake path was clean. At 12 it collapsed. The load
  generators' warmup fence-stormed, with 489 to 735 FENCED rejections per pod,
  and only about 40 percent of entities ever woke, while every tier sat at 12
  percent CPU or less. The numbers are stated identically in three places:
  research doc 05 (`05-proposed-architecture.md` lines 92 to 93), research doc
  07 (`07-jepsen-style-verification.md` lines 75 to 77), and the epic #9
  objective on GitHub.

  <Table>
    | Tier | CPU at collapse |
    | --- | --- |
    | chronicle | 4% |
    | Redis | 4% |
    | Postgres | 12% |
  </Table>

  The cause was not capacity and not the recovery sweep. The agents runtime
  registers one subscription per entity *type*, named `"<type>-handler"`, and
  chronicle holds exactly one single-holder lease per subscription. So every
  entity of a type and every replica hosting one contends for that one lease.
  More replicas means more contenders on one lock, so the queue on the single
  control-plane slot lengthens, heartbeats land late, leases lapse, takeovers
  fence the deposed holder's in-flight work, and the loser retries, which adds
  more operations. Research doc 05 (lines 91 to 96) names the binding
  constraint as lock contention on a single subscription's lease, and adds that
  2.5 times the CPU made it worse, which is the signature of a contention
  collapse rather than a saturation one.

  The job of the rig was to show the inverse of that collapse on real cloud
  infrastructure: that adding replicas no longer fence-storms, that the state
  and sweep cost stay inside budget at scale, and that the claim lease stays
  correct under load and under faults.

  <Callout icon="info" label="One provenance caveat on the original numbers">
    The 489 to 735 FENCED, the roughly 40 percent woke, and the 12 percent CPU
    figures are cited by research doc 05 (line 96) to two Electric-rig files
    that were not in the read set for this page, so they were verified only as
    restated consistently across the three documents above, not opened at the
    primary source. Research doc 05 (line 109) itself notes that the exact
    per-wake interleaving at 12 replicas is inferred from the proven halves plus
    the collapse counts.
  </Callout>
</section>

<section class="block" id="configs">
  <SectionHead num="02" label="The configurations" title="Every System-Under-Test that <em>ran</em>" />

  Five separate campaigns drove chronicle on the cloud. They used different
  node sizes, replica counts, and Redis tiers, and that variation matters
  because some of the differences are exactly what confounds or confirms a
  result. The table below lists each run, its chronicle configuration, its
  Redis, its GKE nodes, and its replica count. The numbers come from each run's
  results file, cited in the rows that follow and in section 04.

  <Table wide>
    | Run | chronicle SUT | Redis | GKE nodes | Replicas |
    | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
    | Sweep-smoke (first shakeout) | 1 replica, 1 CPU / 1 Gi, sweep interval 2s | Memorystore BASIC, 1 GB, REDIS_7_2, noeviction, single node | 1x e2-standard-2 SUT + 1x e2-standard-2 loadgen | 1 |
    | Claude-fleet V&V | 2 replicas, 1 CPU / 1 Gi (downsized from 2 CPU to fit the node) | Memorystore BASIC, 1 GB, single node | 2x e2-standard-2 SUT + 1x e2-standard-2 loadgen | 2 |
    | Codex-fleet V&V | 1 replica, metrics enabled | Memorystore BASIC, 1 GB, single node | 2x e2-standard-2 | 1 |
    | Gate2 Phase 1 (scale curve) | CPU overridden to 1 per pod so N=12 would fit | Standalone Memorystore BASIC | 2x e2-standard-4 loadgen + 3x e2-standard-2 obs; SUT pool quota-exhausted | swept 1, 2, 4, 8 (12 attempted, not run) |
    | Gate2 Phase 2 (contention) | 2 replicas | Standalone Memorystore BASIC | 2x e2-standard-4 loadgen + 3x e2-standard-2 obs | 2 |
    | Gate2 Phase 3 (multi-shard fan-out) | 1 replica, 2 CPU / 2 Gi (intended) | Memorystore CLUSTER, 3 shards (intended; never reached ready) | 2x e2-standard-4 loadgen + 3x e2-standard-2 obs | 1 (intended; not measured) |
    | Gate2 Phase 4 (fair K=10k sweep) | 1 replica, 2 CPU / 2 Gi (the fair size) | Standalone Memorystore BASIC | SUT on e2-standard-4-class capacity + 3x e2-standard-2 obs | 1 |
    | Gate2 Phase 5 (Jepsen) | 2 replicas | Standalone Memorystore BASIC | 2x e2-standard-4 loadgen + 3x e2-standard-2 obs | 2 |
  </Table>

  Two configuration choices drive the results. First, several runs used 1 CPU
  per pod instead of the 2 CPU default, either to fit cost-minimal
  e2-standard-2 nodes or so that 12 replicas could fit on three e2-standard-4
  nodes at 12 vCPU total. The recovery sweep is CPU-bound, so a 1 CPU run is not
  a like-for-like comparison against a 2 CPU baseline, and section 04 shows one
  result that flips between fail and pass purely on this. Second, every
  successful Redis phase ran on Memorystore BASIC, which is a single node with
  no sharding and no managed failover. A single-node Redis has one hash slot
  range on one node, so the cross-node maximum round-trip time that a sharded
  cluster would expose was never on the table for those phases.

  <Callout icon="info" label="One project, suffixed resources">
    Every campaign ran in the GCP project `adityavkk-prototyping`, in region
    `us-central1` and zone `us-central1-a` (`run-gate2.sh` lines 21 to 23). The
    parallel fleets used resource names suffixed `-claude` and `-codex` in the
    same project to keep their clusters and Redis instances separate.
  </Callout>
</section>

<section class="block" id="rig">
  <SectionHead num="03" label="The rig" title="Provision, five phases, <em>teardown to zero</em>" />

  A run is a single shell script, `run-gate2.sh`, that provisions cloud
  infrastructure, drives five phases of load, and then destroys everything. The
  whole cycle is designed to leave no resource running, because a stranded GKE
  cluster keeps billing.

  Provisioning builds the container images for `linux/amd64` through Cloud
  Build into Artifact Registry, because the development machine is an ARM Mac
  with no cross-architecture emulation (`run-gate2.sh` lines 185 to 191). It
  then stands up the cluster and Redis with Terraform in `loadtest/terraform`
  (`run-gate2.sh` line 29). The script enables six GCP APIs first: container,
  redis, artifactregistry, cloudbuild, servicenetworking, and
  networkconnectivity (`run-gate2.sh` lines 172 to 178).

  The five phases run in order, each driven by a purpose-built Go load
  generator (`run-gate2.sh` lines 11 to 16):

  <Table wide>
    | Phase | What it drives | Driver |
    | --- | --- | --- |
    | 1 | Horizontal-scale curve: append fan-out p99 as replicas grow | fanoutscale |
    | 2 | Claim contention: single-holder linearizability at higher worker counts | jepsen single-holder-linz |
    | 3 | Gate #2 cross-node fan-out on a Redis Cluster | fanoutscale |
    | 4 | Recovery sweep at K=10,000 subscriptions | sweepscale |
    | 5 | Jepsen correctness under pod and Redis kills | jepsen-checker |
  </Table>

  The fan-out driver, `fanoutscale`, creates one sentinel stream, seeds the
  subscriptions all linked to it with 32-way concurrency, drives appends at a
  steady rate, and scrapes the `chronicle_fanout_seconds` and
  `chronicle_fanout_subs` metrics from `/metrics` to report fan-out p50, p99,
  and p999 in milliseconds (`fanoutscale/main.go` lines 98 to 257). It exists
  because the earlier `sweepscale` driver seeded subscriptions but drove no
  appends, so `chronicle_fanout_seconds` stayed at a count of zero and the
  fan-out latency was never populated (`fanoutscale/main.go` lines 4 to 7).

  Teardown is mandatory and runs on every exit path. The script installs a trap
  on the EXIT, INT, and TERM signals (`run-gate2.sh` line 84), which runs a
  Terraform destroy of the GKE cluster and both the standalone and cluster Redis
  instances, then verifies that no clusters remain in the region
  (`run-gate2.sh` lines 49 to 83). The cost estimate is about 3 dollars per
  hour, with a total under 80 dollars (`run-gate2.sh` line 18). Phase 5 uses a
  self-restarting port-forward loop because the Jepsen nemesis kills pods, which
  would otherwise break a static port-forward (`run-gate2.sh` lines 108 to 125).

  <Callout icon="info" label="Teardown confirmed at zero ongoing cost">
    Every campaign confirmed teardown left nothing running. The gate2 trap
    completed its destroy (`run-gate2.sh` lines 81 to 82). The claude-fleet run
    recorded clusters none, Memorystore none, and zero dollars ongoing, verified
    with `gcloud container clusters list` and `gcloud redis instances list`
    against its `-claude`-suffixed resources. The codex run recorded the same
    zero-ongoing result for its `-codex`-suffixed resources.
  </Callout>
</section>

<section class="block" id="results">
  <SectionHead num="04" label="The results" title="Flat fan-out, a sweep floor, <em>a contention knee</em>" />

  Three measured gates passed on the cluster `chronicle-gate2` on 2026-06-22,
  recorded in `loadtest/RESULTS-gate2.md`. They are the inverse of the original
  collapse. A fourth gate, the one that would have been the most decisive, did
  not run.

  ### The headline: a flat fan-out curve

  Phase 1 is the fence-storm regression test. It ramps replicas through 1, 2,
  4, and 8, with 4 pull-wake subscriptions, an append rate of 10 per second, a
  20 second warmup, and a 60 second measurement window, port-forwarded to the
  first pod for each replica count (`RESULTS-gate2.md` lines 13 to 28). The
  append-to-wake p99 is flat.

  <Table wide>
    | Replicas N | fanout p99 (ms) | fanout p50 (ms) | subs per fanout | append errors | Verdict |
    | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
    | 1 | 12.48 | 5.20 | 4 | 0 | PASS |
    | 2 | 12.61 | 5.59 | 4 | 0 | PASS |
    | 4 | 12.58 | 5.45 | 4 | 0 | PASS |
    | 8 | 12.53 | 5.29 | 4 | 0 | PASS |
  </Table>

  The p99 holds at 12.5 milliseconds plus or minus 0.1 across the range, a
  spread of 0.13 milliseconds while the replica count quadruples, with zero
  append errors at every count. If the single control-plane slot were still
  serializing under concurrency, the p99 would climb as replicas grow. It does
  not. The original failure showed fence-storming that worsened with more
  replicas and worsened further with more CPU. Here the curve is flat.

  The downside of this result is that the absolute number is not meaningful and
  the failing replica count was not reached. The p99 includes about 10
  milliseconds of port-forward round-trip time, so the file's claim is about
  flatness across N, not the absolute 12.5 milliseconds (`RESULTS-gate2.md`
  lines 16 to 17). Phase 1 also used 1 CPU per pod, not the 2 CPU default, so
  that 12 replicas could fit on the available nodes, which means it measures
  relative latency against N and not absolute throughput. And N=12, the exact
  count that originally collapsed, was not run, which section 05 covers.

  ### The K=10,000 regression floor

  Phase 4 measures the recovery sweep at 10,000 subscriptions with 5 links each,
  at the fair node size of 2 CPU on e2-standard-4-class capacity
  (`RESULTS-gate2.md` lines 53 to 60). The limit is a sweep tick p99 under 1500
  milliseconds.

  <Table wide>
    | Run | CPU | K | Links per sub | sweep p50 (ms) | sweep p99 (ms) | Verdict |
    | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
    | Sweep-smoke (single link) | 1 | 10,000 | 1 | 384 | 509 | PASS |
    | Claude-fleet | 1 | 10,000 | 5 | 1536 | 2037.8 | FAIL (confounded) |
    | Gate2 Phase 4 (fair) | 2 | 10,000 | 5 | 768 | 1019 | PASS |
  </Table>

  The fair re-run passed at 1019 milliseconds, seeding all 10,000 subscriptions
  in 4.1 seconds with zero seed errors and running 60 sweep ticks in 120
  seconds. The claude-fleet run failed the same gate at 2037.8 milliseconds, but
  that run had been shrunk from 2 CPU to 1 CPU to fit cost-minimal nodes, and
  the sweep is CPU-bound because it reads many slots and tails per tick. The
  fair re-run at 2 CPU resolves the confound: the failure was the 1 CPU sizing,
  not a code regression (`RESULTS-gate2.md` lines 53 to 60 and 94). The earlier
  509 millisecond single-link result from the first shakeout is the standing
  baseline that every later campaign cites (`RESULTS-gke.md` lines 7 to 46), and
  the downside to comparing against it is that it used 1 link per subscription
  while the gate uses 5, so the 1019 millisecond figure is the right
  apples-to-apples result at 5 links and the 509 millisecond figure is a lighter
  single-link control.

  ### The contention knee

  Phase 2 checks that the single-holder claim lease stays linearizable as the
  number of concurrent claim workers rises, using the jepsen
  single-holder-linz checker over a 12,000 millisecond workload
  (`RESULTS-gate2.md` lines 30 to 37).

  <Table wide>
    | Workers W | Operations | Claims | Granted | Linearizable | Verdict |
    | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
    | 4 | 451 | 417 | 34 | yes | PASS |
    | 16 | 1267 | 1225 | 42 | yes | PASS |
  </Table>

  The claim is linearizable at both 4 and 16 workers. This is the G=1 point, the
  claim granularity of one lease per type, which is the granularity that
  originally collapsed. The downside is what this cloud phase does not test. It
  measures the single-lease G=1 design at higher worker counts on one healthy
  node, so it does not on its own show the knee moving. The finer-grained claim
  fix the original collapse calls for is built and green locally, not pending:
  the per-(subId,g) claim split lands in `webhook/shard.go` (FNV-1a, default
  G=16) and the contention suite proves the knee moved. On a real Chronicle plus
  Redis stack the `contention` scenario (jepsen/checker `scenario_contention.go`)
  shows that at G=16 per-worker throughput holds near 27 while aggregate scales
  158 to 336 to 650, while G=1 collapses, so the C2 knee that broke G=1 at N=12
  moved beyond the whole G=16 ramp (C1 to C3 and doc-05 gate #6 GREEN,
  `docs/jepsen/results.md` lines 214 to 234). What stays cloud-pending is the
  multi-node reproduction of that knee under scale, blocked by quota, not the
  mechanism itself.

  ### Gate #2: the number that was never measured

  Phase 3 was meant to be the deciding gate. It would measure the append fan-out
  p99 at 2, 4, 8, and 256 subscribers on a 3-shard Memorystore Cluster, which is
  the sharded substrate where slots span nodes and a cross-node maximum
  round-trip time appears. It did not run.

  <Table wide>
    | Subscribers S | Substrate | fan-out p99 | Verdict |
    | --- | --- | --- | --- |
    | 2 | Memorystore Cluster (3 shards) | not measured | SKIPPED |
    | 4 | Memorystore Cluster (3 shards) | not measured | SKIPPED |
    | 8 | Memorystore Cluster (3 shards) | not measured | SKIPPED |
    | 256 | Memorystore Cluster (3 shards) | not measured | SKIPPED |
  </Table>

  The Memorystore Cluster never reached a ready state. Provisioning failed with
  an insufficient project quota error on every attempt, twice, each time
  entering a create-then-delete loop and never becoming ready
  (`RESULTS-gate2.md` lines 40 to 51 and 93). The standalone Redis phases were
  unaffected. This is the single most important unmeasured number across all
  campaigns, because the cross-node fan-out latency is what decides whether the
  built slot-homing layout is affordable to keep enabled in production, and
  Memorystore BASIC is single-shard so its slots never span nodes. The fan-out
  mechanism itself is built and proven correct locally: T5, the slot-isolation
  differential checker (jepsen/checker `scenario_slot.go`), is GREEN, with 320
  subscriptions spread across 204 of 256 slots, the scatter-gather subscriber set
  equal to the independent reference and to the brute-force union, zero foreign
  wakes, and a mis-tag detected as CROSSSLOT. What single-node Redis erases is the
  max-node round-trip time, which is the only thing gate #2 reads, so the verdict
  it gates is enable-versus-defer for an already-built and already-correct design,
  not whether the design exists. Section 05 records this as a hard limit on the
  claim.
</section>

<section class="block" id="faults">
  <SectionHead num="05" label="Under faults" title="Killed mid-flight, <em>and a bug found</em>" />

  Phase 5 ran a Jepsen nemesis against 2 replicas, killing origins and using a
  self-restarting port-forward so a second pod served traffic while one was
  killed (`RESULTS-gate2.md` lines 62 to 99). It ran three scenarios that work
  without a cluster-to-host webhook back-channel, which the GKE network did not
  allow. Two passed and one failed.

  <Table wide>
    | Scenario | What it checks | Result |
    | --- | --- | --- |
    | expired-lease-takeover | a deposed worker's late ack is fenced | PASS |
    | single-holder-linz | the claim lease is linearizable under a GC-pause nemesis | PASS |
    | pull-wake-arm-crash | a pull-wake cursor recovers after an origin crash | FAIL, then fixed |
  </Table>

  The lease fence passed cleanly. In expired-lease-takeover, Worker A claimed
  generation 1, stalled 2.5 seconds past its lease time-to-live, Worker B took
  over at generation 2, and Worker A's late ack returned a 409 FENCED rather
  than a silent success. In single-holder-linz at 4 workers, 440 operations were
  linearizable. So the fence rotates correctly on a stale-lease takeover and
  the claim is linearizable under fault injection.

  The third scenario found a real bug. In pull-wake-arm-crash the driver
  appended 120 messages across 6 streams, all 120 reachable in Redis, then the
  nemesis killed all origins once and killed one origin twelve more times. After
  a 120 second settle the subscription cursor was stuck at offset
  `000000000150` for all 6 streams while the stream tail had advanced to
  `000000000293` (`RESULTS-gate2.md` lines 80 to 87). About 143 messages were
  never acked. The root cause was that the recovery sweep did not re-emit
  pull-wake wake events after an origin restart. Pull-wake arms no lease, so the
  lease-expiry path never fires, and a subscription stuck in the waking phase
  with a durably emitted but unclaimed wake had no exit, so it wedged
  permanently and later appends only coalesced against it.

  The bug was fixed in commit `5b99bb9` and re-verified. The fix adds an
  emit-without-claim recovery block to `sweepOnce` in `webhook/manager.go`
  (lines 587 to 599): when a pull-wake subscription has been in the waking phase
  with a sent wake for longer than three sweep intervals, the sweep re-emits the
  same generation and wake id, and a duplicate event with the same fence token
  is claim-safe. It also fixes the jepsen checker, whose drain worker had sent
  zero stream acks, so cursors never advanced. The re-run of
  `jepsen-checker -scenario pull-wake-arm-crash -settle 120s` passed with 6 of 6
  streams reaching their tail under 47 nemesis actions, with no regression on
  the two scenarios that already passed and with the full test suite green.

  <Callout icon="alert" label="The results file shows this as FAIL">
    `RESULTS-gate2.md` was committed before the fix landed and was never updated,
    so it still records pull-wake-arm-crash as FAIL. The re-verified pass lives in
    the commit message for `5b99bb9` and in the closing comments of GitHub issue
    #24, roughly one hour later, not in the results file. Anyone reading only the
    results file would see the bug marked open.
  </Callout>
</section>

<section class="block" id="limits">
  <SectionHead num="06" label="The limits" title="What quota <em>blocked</em>" />

  Two of the numbers this campaign set out to measure were never measured,
  because the GCP project ran out of quota. These are measurement gaps, things
  that did not run, not observed defects. They bound the claim, and the honest
  statement is that the fence-storm-gone verdict rests on a flat trend through 8
  replicas, not on a passing run at the replica count that originally failed.

  <Table wide>
    | Gap | What was blocked | Why | What it bounds |
    | --- | --- | --- | --- |
    | N=12 not re-run | the 12 replica point on the scale curve | insufficient project quota on the SUT node pool | the curve is flat 1 to 8; the count that collapsed was not re-tested |
    | Gate #2 multi-shard fan-out skipped | cross-node fan-out p99 at S=2, 4, 8, 256 | the Redis Cluster never reached ready under insufficient project quota | the viability of spreading subscriptions across slots is unproven |
  </Table>

  The first gap is the direct one. The original collapse was at 12 replicas, and
  Phase 1 measured only 1 through 8 because GCP returned an insufficient project
  quota error on the SUT node pool (`RESULTS-gate2.md` line 28). The script does
  attempt 12, but the pool was exhausted, so the SUT pods were rescheduled onto
  the loadgen and observation nodes for that run. The file's own claim is that
  the trend through 8 is conclusive, not that 12 passed.

  The second gap is the deeper one. Gate #2, the cross-node fan-out latency on a
  sharded cluster, is described in the research as the number that decides
  whether the built slot-homing layout stays enabled in production or is deferred
  to the single-slot ownership build. It has no measured value in any campaign,
  on any rig, because no run ever stood up a multi-shard Redis. The claude-fleet
  run could not populate the fan-out metric at all because its driver seeded
  subscriptions but drove no appends, and the codex run reached an S=256 result
  from only 20 observations rather than a full 100 appends, which is not a valid
  gate. So the headline cross-node fan-out p99 stays unknown. The slot-homing it
  measures is already built and locally correct (T5 GREEN), so this is a missing
  production-readiness number on a shipped mechanism, not a test of an unbuilt
  one.

  Several further gaps follow from the same constraints. The webhook-based
  Jepsen scenarios, baseline and origin-restart, were skipped because they need
  a cluster-to-host back-channel the GKE network did not provide
  (`RESULTS-gate2.md` lines 69 to 70). Gates #3, #4, and #5 test mechanisms that
  do exist on the main branch (the due-set outbox, leased slot ownership over the
  `{ownership}` keyspace, and the failover-fence drill, all merged by PR #18 and
  green in the local suite), but their cloud numbers were not measured because
  each needs at least two replicas plus chaos on a clean multi-node rig, and the
  same quota that blocked the cluster blocked that rig. The acceptance gates for
  those mechanisms passed locally already: gate #4's ownership exclusivity (T3)
  is linearizable on a real Redis, and the L3 failover recovery is proven
  in-process. The recommended follow-up is to request a quota increase for
  `redis.googleapis.com` in `adityavkk-prototyping`, then re-run Phase 3 in
  isolation, and to re-run Phase 1 with node pool quota for 12 replicas.

  <Callout icon="info" label="The verdict, stated plainly">
    The slot-homed, work-sharded design is built and green locally (T1 to T5,
    L1, L3, and C1 to C3 with doc-05 gate #6 all pass on a real Chronicle plus
    Redis stack). Deployed on cloud on a single-slot substrate, that binary no
    longer fence-storms as replicas grow from 1 to 8, holds its recovery sweep
    inside budget at 10,000 subscriptions, and keeps its claim lease
    linearizable up to 16 workers. It found and fixed one real recovery bug along
    the way. On cloud it did not re-prove the 12 replica point that originally
    failed, and it did not measure the cross-node, multi-shard fan-out latency
    that decides whether the built slot-homing stays enabled in production. Both,
    along with the L2, L4, and L5 multi-node liveness gates, are blocked on GCP
    project quota, and until that quota is granted, the cloud claim is bounded to
    the range that was actually measured.
  </Callout>
